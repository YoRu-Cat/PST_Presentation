01
Book and editor

The book treats Zia as a coalition builder, not just a dictator.

Craig Baxter's 1985 volume frames Zia's regime through interest groups: military officers, clerics, urban elites, rural notables, Afghan refugees, and the broader economy. The point is not personality alone, but the way power was assembled and maintained.

The presentation is built around that same structure: each section shows how a pressure point, a patron, or a policy choice kept the regime standing.
Westview Press, 1985 Edited by Craig Baxter Interest group model Multi-author analysis

02
Historical context

1977 was the rupture that made the rest of the regime possible.

Bhutto's disputed election, the PNA agitation, the 5 July coup, the suspension of politics, and then the Soviet invasion of Afghanistan created the environment Zia needed. What begins as crisis management quickly becomes a long-term authoritarian arrangement.

1977Election crisis, street protests, and the Operation Fair Play coup.
1978Zia consolidates the presidency and extends martial rule.
1979Bhutto is executed; the Soviet invasion turns Pakistan into a frontline state.
1985Partyless elections provide a controlled return to civilian institutions.

03
Transformation agenda

Islamization, controlled politics, and foreign policy shift formed the regime's spine.

Zia's signature move was not only repression. It was rebranding the state: Nizam-i-Mustafa, shariah benches, zakat, Hudood ordinances, party bans, local bodies, and a foreign policy that leaned hard into the anti-Soviet front. Ideology and pragmatism were fused into one machine.

Islamization

Shariah courts, zakat, Islamic banking, and the claim that state power was being made morally legitimate.

Control

Party bans, Majlis-e-Shura, local bodies, and partyless elections that kept opposition fragmented.

Foreign policy

Pakistan becomes indispensable in the Afghan war and earns massive aid and strategic cover.

04
Economy and social base

Growth and remittances bought time.

The deck's economic argument is blunt: average growth near 6 percent, large Gulf remittances, and a broad enough distribution of gains gave the regime breathing room. It was not an economic miracle, but it was enough to keep major constituencies from exploding at once.

  • Remittances from migrant workers stabilized households and softened labor unrest.
  • Urban elites benefited from denationalization and freer circulation of goods.
  • Rural notables stayed inside the system through subsidies and local bodies.
  • Low domestic savings and infrastructure deficits remained structural weaknesses.
Economic luck did not erase political problems, but it made discontent easier to postpone.

05
Military and security

The army became both the guardrail and the beneficiary.

Under Zia, military expansion reached deep into civilian administration. Officers filled government posts, defense spending rose, military-linked business grew, and new recruits came from more conservative middle-class backgrounds. At the same time, the Soviet border threat made military rule easier to justify.

ArmyMore personnel, more influence, more overlap with civil administration.
Frontline stateAfghanistan turned internal politics into a security story.
Nuclear trackStrategic competition with India continued under military control.

06
Afghan refugees

The refugee crisis was both burden and leverage.

The Soviet invasion produced millions of Afghan refugees, with Pakistan absorbing enormous pressure in Khyber Pakhtunkhwa and Balochistan. The regime used this to reinforce its frontline-state identity, justify martial law, and unlock aid, even as local labor and services came under strain.

  • Refugees concentrated in border provinces and around Peshawar.
  • International aid offset some fiscal stress and strengthened Zia's position.
  • Drug and arms smuggling expanded along the conflict corridor.
  • Spillover risk made security politics more central than democratic reform.

07
Coalition building

Zia survived by rewarding allies and preventing a unified opposition.

Baxter's conclusion is that Zia was effective at assembling a layered coalition: clerics got influence, officers got posts and benefits, rural notables got local bodies, and urban elites got economic pragmatism. Repression existed, but it was calibrated to avoid creating one giant anti-regime bloc.

Clerics

Islamization gave ideological legitimacy and positions inside the state.

Military

Benefits, promotions, and civil slots made the army invested in continuity.

Elites

Landlords and industrialists were brought back in through denationalization and access.

Image source: Wikimedia Commons
08
Legitimacy

Accepted, but never fully legitimized.

The legitimacy story is split. Zia had economic performance, religious messaging, and security fears on his side. Yet he never won a free national election, and major professional and political groups remained alienated. The regime was tolerated more than embraced.

  • 1984 referendum and 1985 elections gave procedural cover, not full democratic consent.
  • Lawyers, journalists, and many urban professionals stayed oppositional.
  • Sindh remained especially hostile because of Bhutto's execution.
  • The state looked stable, but stability relied on managed contradiction.
Circumstances were kind to Zia, but kindness is not the same thing as legitimacy.
Image source: Wikimedia Commons
09
Future outlook

The authors warn that the model could break if growth, aid, or coalition discipline collapsed.

The outlook section matters because it shows the regime's vulnerabilities clearly: declining remittances, rural unrest, Sindhi alienation, Baluch grievances, and regional security shocks. Zia's system could work only while the balance of pressure stayed manageable.

RiskIf growth falters and distribution stops feeling broad enough, the coalition can unravel quickly.
ScenarioControlled transition was possible, but only if the military kept the veto and the opposition stayed fragmented.
Core warningA frontier state can look strong while resting on very fragile assumptions.

Closing synthesis

Baxter's verdict is simple: Zia survived because he managed the system better than his rivals managed opposition.

The regime endured not through charisma, but through circumstance, coalition discipline, military power, religious legitimacy, and economic luck. It was accepted more than loved, stabilized more than democratized, and preserved by a world that treated Pakistan as strategically too important to fail.
